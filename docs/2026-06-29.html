US Partially Lifts Anthropic Export Ban: Mythos 5 Cleared for ~100 US Orgs, Fable 5 Still Offline

The Commerce Department on June 26 cleared Claude Mythos 5 for a vetted list of roughly 100 US companies and federal agencies, two weeks after a national-security export directive suspended worldwide access to both Mythos 5 and Fable 5. Fable 5, Anthropic's general-purpose frontier model launched publicly June 9 at $10/$50 per million tokens, remains blocked globally.

Why it matters — The first selective restoration of a commercially-banned AI model sets precedent for frontier AI governance; Fable 5's continued absence signals the standoff with the government is unresolved.

US Export Controls Cut Global Dev Teams Off From Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5

A government export-control directive on June 12 suspended all non-US access to Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5, including foreign-national Anthropic employees. Mythos 5 was partially restored for roughly 100 vetted US organizations on June 26–27; Fable 5 — Anthropic's strongest publicly released model — remains offline globally.

Why it matters — Teams outside the US using Fable 5 or Mythos 5 via Claude Code or the API have lost access; Anthropic says applying this standard industry-wide would effectively halt all new frontier model deployments.

Zhipu AI's GLM-5.2 Matches Claude Mythos on Cybersecurity Benchmarks — Open-Weight, MIT License

On June 28, Zhipu AI disclosed that GLM-5.2, released open-weight under an MIT license on June 13, matches Claude Mythos on automated security bug-detection benchmarks — the same capability class cited as the national-security justification for the June 12 US export ban. Unlike Anthropic's model, GLM-5.2 can be downloaded, fine-tuned, or stripped of safety controls by anyone globally.

Why it matters — The export control's containment logic is undercut: a parity-level open-source model is now freely accessible worldwide regardless of whether Anthropic's API is live.

npm v12 Ships in July: Install Scripts Blocked by Default, Breaking Most CI Pipelines

npm v12, due July 2026, defaults allowScripts to off, blocks git dependencies, and blocks remote-URL tarball dependencies — all three require explicit opt-in. The changes are available now behind warnings in npm 11.16.0+; teams can audit with npm approve-scripts --allow-scripts-pending before the release.

Why it matters — Most Node.js CI pipelines need explicit config updates before v12 ships; teams using native addons (node-gyp) or git-sourced packages must act before July.
